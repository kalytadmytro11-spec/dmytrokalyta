Porto / Fintech · Crypto Exchange / Identity · Mobile · Web

Safety and simple bitcoin transactions — to anyone, anywhere.

A peer-to-peer crypto exchange that lets people buy bitcoin directly from each other using their local payment method. I owned identity, marketing site, and the mobile application from concept to production-ready spec.

01 — Context

A crypto product that needed to feel trustworthy first.

Porto is a peer-to-peer crypto marketplace — people buy and sell bitcoin directly between each other, using their local payment method (bank card, transfer, even cash). The team came to me with a working backend and a clear thesis: most crypto apps look like trading terminals; ours has to look like a banking app.

Two audiences mattered. New users coming to crypto for the first time, who needed everything to feel calm and obvious. And experienced traders who'd churn instantly if anything felt slow or amateur. The brand had to bridge both.

02 — Approach

Three deliverables, one system.

PHASE 01 · IDENTITY

Wordmark & visual system

Built the PORTO wordmark with a custom "O" treatment and a warm amber-to-orange palette anchored by dark navy ink. The mark works at favicon scale and on physical signage.

PHASE 02 · MOBILE

iOS & Android app

Wallet, transaction list, send/receive, P2P marketplace, payment-method picker, and QR-based payouts. Designed to be readable in one-thumb use and translate cleanly across iOS and Android.

PHASE 03 · WEB

Marketing & marketplace

Hero, value props, payment-method matrix, "what is Porto" walkthrough, customer testimonials, and download CTAs — all built from the same component library as the app.

03 — Decisions

Three choices that shaped the whole product.

Yellow as a calm signal, not a warning. Most fintech defaults to blue or green; we used a warm amber as the brand's primary, paired with a deep navy. This made the product feel friendly without losing the "money is serious" register.

The transaction list as the centre of the app. Not the chart. Not the price ticker. The actual list of "did this happen, when, for how much" — because that's what every user actually came to check.

Payment methods as first-class objects. Most P2P crypto products bury payment choice in a dropdown. We surfaced it as a primary filter — because the deciding factor for buyers is "can I actually pay you", not "what price are you offering."

05 — Reflection

What I learned.

Building a brand and a product in parallel is fragile — every brand decision pulled the UI, every UI decision pulled the brand back. The thing that saved us was committing to one shared component library early, even before either layer was "done." Components became the negotiation surface, not Slack threads.

The other lesson: in regulated/financial categories, the perception of trust does most of the heavy lifting. Spacing, alignment, and restraint moved the trust needle further than any clever interaction. Sometimes good design is just not doing the show-offy thing.
