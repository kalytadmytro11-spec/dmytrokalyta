Arcade Machine Shop / E-commerce · Landing Page / Web · Responsive

A landing page that feels like walking into the arcade.

Single-page e-commerce design for Arcade Machine Shop — a retailer selling restored Pac-Man, Galaga, pinball, and bartop cabinets. The site had to evoke the smoky-glow nostalgia of an arcade hall while still working as a fast, modern, mobile-first storefront.

Role
UI / UX Designer

01 — Context

A niche store that needed to feel its niche.

Arcade Machine Shop sells genuinely interesting hardware — restored cabinets, pinball machines, accessories — to a hyper-specific audience of collectors, bar owners, and serious enthusiasts. The product photography is gorgeous. The category is inherently visual. The previous site looked like a generic Shopify template.

The brief from the client was direct: "It should feel like walking into the arcade." Hot lights, dark room, the product glowing. But it also has to work — clear inventory, transparent pricing, real CTAs, and a checkout that converts on phone.

02 — Approach

One scrollable page, four jobs.

SECTION 01 · HERO

Identity-driven intro

Split-frame hero — heavy-key character photo on one side, dark ink on the other, with the brand mark and a single primary CTA. The product world is the first thing on screen.

SECTION 02 · STORY

About + key facts

"The largest arcade supplier" with three credibility stats (5 years delivering fun, 20,483 machines sold, 99% satisfied customers) — answering trust questions before the product grid starts.

SECTION 03 · CATALOGUE

Four product rails

Arcade Machines, Pinball, Countercades, Accessories — each in a four-card horizontal rail with name, price, and a one-click "Learn more". Designed to be scanned, not read.

"It should feel like walking into the arcade — hot lights, dark room, the product glowing."

03 — Decisions

Three choices that defined the experience.

Photo-first, copy-second. Every product rail leads with the cabinet — full bleed, atmospheric lighting, isolated on a dark surface. Specs and price come below. This matches how collectors actually shop: visual recognition first, decision later.

One CTA per section, never more. Hero CTA, product CTA, newsletter CTA. Each section earns the user's attention by being decisively about one thing. The site never asks the user to choose between two things at the same time.

Mobile gets the full experience, not a stripped-down one. The mobile layout preserves the same atmosphere, the same imagery scale, the same product detail. It's just stacked. Niche-product buyers don't tolerate a "lite" version of the catalogue.

04 — Deliverables

What was shipped.

Page sections
7

Hero, about/stats, arcade machines, pinball, countercades, accessories, newsletter — each with its own visual rhythm.

Breakpoints
3

Desktop, tablet, and mobile — designed in parallel so the experience never collapses into the worst-case responsive layout.

Product detail
1·tmpl

Modal-based product detail with image, specs, "Buy Now" and "Reviews / Specs" tabs — designed to slot into the existing checkout flow.

05 — Reflection

What I learned.

With niche-passion products, the brand cannot be neutral. A neutral, "safe" e-commerce design tells the audience the seller doesn't really care — and that's a disqualifier for collectors. Committing to a strong, atmospheric direction was the most important early call.

I also re-learned how much a single great hero image can do. The split-frame intro carries the entire emotional load of the brand. Get that one image right, and the rest of the site can be quiet and functional — exactly as it should be.
